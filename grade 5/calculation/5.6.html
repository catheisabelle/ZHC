Brackets, Order of Operations, and Arithmetic Laws

Let’s learn how to use brackets to order operations and apply arithmetic laws!

Brackets are used to group numbers and operations, helping us solve math problems in a clear order.

What are Brackets?

Brackets (also called parentheses) are symbols used to group numbers and operations in a math problem. They tell you which part of the problem to solve first.

Example: In the expression (3 + 5) × 2, you must solve 3 + 5 first because it is inside the brackets.

Order of Operations (PEMDAS)

PEMDAS is the order you follow when solving math problems:

  • P: Parentheses (solve inside brackets first)
  • E: Exponents (powers or square roots)
  • MD: Multiplication and Division (from left to right)
  • AS: Addition and Subtraction (from left to right)

Example: (2 + 3) × 4 - 6 ÷ 2

  1. Step 1: Solve inside the brackets: (2 + 3) = 5
  2. Step 2: Multiply 5 by 4 = 20
  3. Step 3: Divide 6 by 2 = 3
  4. Step 4: Subtract 3 from 20 = 17
Commutative Property

The commutative property tells us that the order of numbers doesn’t matter for addition and multiplication.

  • Addition: 3 + 5 = 5 + 3
  • Multiplication: 4 × 2 = 2 × 4

You can swap the numbers around in addition and multiplication without changing the result.

Associative Property

The associative property tells us that the way numbers are grouped doesn’t matter for addition and multiplication.

  • Addition: (2 + 3) + 4 = 2 + (3 + 4)
  • Multiplication: (5 × 2) × 3 = 5 × (2 × 3)

You can change how numbers are grouped in addition and multiplication without changing the result.

Distributive Property

The distributive property tells us how multiplication distributes over addition.

Example: 3 × (4 + 5) = (3 × 4) + (3 × 5)

Multiply 3 by both 4 and 5, then add the results.
